# Molina Healthcare: Company Overview

## What Molina Is

Molina Healthcare (NYSE: MOH) is a FORTUNE 500 managed care organization that contracts with state governments to administer Medicaid and Medicare benefits to low-income and dual-eligible populations. As of Q1 2026, Molina serves approximately 5.0 million members across 19 states, with Medicaid representing 83.2% of total enrollment — making it the most Medicaid-concentrated of the "Big Five" managed care organizations. Unlike peers such as Centene or UnitedHealth Group, Molina has no material commercial insurance business; it is almost entirely a government-program insurer.

|---|---|---|---|
| Premium Revenue | $10.2B | $43.1B | ~$42B |
| Total Members | 5.03M | 5.49M | ~5.1M (year-end) |
| Medicaid MCR | 92.0% | 91.8% | 92.9% |

| Adjusted EPS | $2.35 | $11.03 | ≥$5.00 |
| Days in Claims Payable | 44 | 47 | — |

**Medical Care Ratio (MCR)** — also called MLR (Medical Loss Ratio) — is the fraction of premium revenue paid out as medical claims. A Medicaid MCR of 92% means 92 cents of every dollar Molina receives goes to paying member healthcare costs. CMS sets a federal MLR floor of 85% under 42 CFR §438.74, and states can negotiate higher floors in their managed care contracts; Molina must stay above the applicable floor while staying below a level that would eliminate profit. Every 100 basis points on the Medicaid MCR is worth approximately $5 per share in earnings, per CEO Joe Zubretsky — making this the single most watched number in the business.

## The Strategic Situation in 2026

### 2026 Is a Trough Year

Molina management has explicitly called 2026 a "trough year" for Medicaid margins. The core problem is a rate-trend imbalance: state capitation rates have not kept pace with rising medical costs. Molina estimates the Medicaid market is "underfunded by 300–400 basis points." The company still expects low single-digit positive Medicaid margins — not losses — and views rate restoration as inevitable through actuarial soundness requirements.

### Medicaid Enrollment Under Pressure

Two distinct forces are reducing Molina's Medicaid membership in 2026:

1. **Post-redetermination acuity shift**: Following the pandemic-era continuous coverage pause, states conducted eligibility redeterminations from mid-2023 through 2024. National Medicaid enrollment fell ~20% from 86.7M (March 2023) to 69.5M (October 2025). Molina lost 5.5% of Medicaid enrollment since March 2023 — the smallest percentage decline among the Big Five, which management attributes to its lower exposure to states with aggressive redetermination timelines.

2. **Trump administration policy**: H.R. 1 (July 2025) cuts Medicaid enrollment; the CBO projects 11.4 million coverage losses by 2034. In Q1 2026, Molina lost more Medicaid members than expected — particularly in California (immigrants without legal status), Illinois, New York, and Texas. Molina now forecasts a 6% full-year Medicaid membership decline for 2026, up from its earlier 2% estimate.

The key risk from disenrollment: if the members who leave are relatively healthy, those who remain are sicker on average (higher acuity), which pushes claims costs up without a corresponding rate increase. Molina management argues this acuity risk is largely behind them, citing data that low/no-utilizer membership is already at historic lows.

### Medicare Advantage Exit

Molina is exiting its Medicare Advantage Part D (MAPD) product for 2027. The MAPD business (~$1B in annual premium) dragged down earnings; Molina took a $93M impairment charge in Q1 2026 related to the planned exit. The company will retain its dual-eligible (Medicare-Medicaid) business, which remains core to its strategy.

## Competitive Context: The Big Five

The "Big Five" Medicaid MCOs — Centene, CVS/Aetna, Elevance, Molina, UnitedHealth — together control roughly half the Medicaid managed care market. In Q4 2025:

- **Molina's Medicaid revenue** grew +27.0% since the start of the unwinding (March 2023), despite enrollment declines — driven by higher capitation rates and rising member acuity.
- **Centene and Elevance** had the steepest enrollment losses (-23% and -28.5% respectively); Molina had the smallest at -5.5%.
- Molina's Medicaid MCR of 93.5% in Q4 2025 was the highest among the Big Five that reported the metric, reflecting cost pressure.

The pattern across all five companies: enrollment declining, revenue per member rising (acuity + rate increases), margins under pressure. Molina is the most exposed because Medicaid is 83% of its business, leaving less diversification against Medicaid headwinds.

## What This Means for a Claims Data Analyst at Molina

- **MCR management is the central analytical problem.** Every claims analysis ultimately connects back to whether Molina is managing medical spending within actuarially sound rate structures.
- **Medicaid enrollment volatility** creates challenges for predictive modeling — member acuity shifts as populations churn.
- **Days in Claims Payable (44–47 days)** is a liquidity signal analysts watch; it measures how quickly Molina is settling claims obligations.
- **State-level rate negotiations** mean analysts need to understand not just aggregate trends but state-by-state cost variation, since each state contract has different rate structures and risk corridors.
